# Game Constitution

## Purpose
This document exists to protect the identity of MargotGameOfLife as it grows. It defines the permanent conceptual rules of the game so new content can be added for years without replacing the core worldview, collapsing readability, or forcing mechanical resets.

## One-Sentence Thesis
MargotGameOfLife is a private parent-child co-op village life sim where effort, judgment, saving, ownership, and civic contribution turn into visible improvements in life, place, and future possibility.

## Permanent Design Pillars
- **Value must be made.** Useful things should come from effort, skill, judgment, and time rather than from arbitrary reward fountains.
- **Money is fuel, not the goal.** Currency matters because it unlocks better living, better tools, better choices, and better shared places.
- **Ownership must change tomorrow.** Productive assets should alter future options, not just decorate an inventory.
- **Stewardship must be visible.** Care, neglect, patience, and contribution should change how the world looks and functions.
- **Society must be concrete.** Shared life should be felt through people, places, dependencies, and public works, not through abstract moral messaging.
- **Depth must layer, not replace.** The same systems should stay understandable to a child while revealing deeper meaning with experience.

## Permanent Verbs
- **Gather**: take materials, goods, or opportunities from the world through time and attention.
- **Make**: turn inputs into something more useful, valuable, beautiful, or nourishing.
- **Trade**: exchange with villagers, markets, or institutions to convert work into options.
- **Save**: delay immediate satisfaction in order to reach a better future state.
- **Own**: acquire and steward tools, assets, or places that improve future life.
- **Contribute**: give resources, labor, or priority to shared household or civic outcomes.
- **Judge**: compare options, read trade-offs, and live with consequences.

## Permanent Entities
- **Item**: any portable or countable thing, including materials, foods, tools, currency, deeds, and crafted goods.
- **Recipe**: any repeatable transformation of inputs, place, time, and effort into outputs.
- **Asset**: any owned thing that keeps producing value, access, convenience, beauty, or freedom over time.
- **Place**: any meaningful location where work, trade, living, or civic life happens.
- **Villager**: any social actor with needs, opinions, requests, and partial perspective.
- **Project**: any shared work that advances through contribution and changes the world when completed.
- **Commission**: any request, order, or task that turns local needs into economic or moral choice.
- **Event**: any temporary condition or dilemma that pressures existing systems without replacing them.

## Core Loop Families
### Production Loop
Players gather or acquire inputs, make something better from them, and trade the result. This loop proves that effort and craft create value and gives the game its clearest, most repeatable baseline.

### Ownership Loop
Players save instead of spending everything now, then acquire an asset that improves future output, convenience, beauty, or freedom. This loop teaches why productive ownership matters without turning the game into abstract finance.

### Stewardship Loop
Players care for assets, places, and routines over time so life stays productive, legible, and pleasant. This loop makes maintenance meaningful and keeps ownership from becoming passive number accumulation.

### Civic Loop
Players contribute to shared projects that visibly improve access, safety, beauty, or opportunity for everyone using the place. This loop makes society tangible by turning sacrifice into a changed world.

### Judgment Loop
Players interpret requests, advice, incentives, timing, and incomplete information, then choose what matters most. This loop is where critical thinking enters the game and where shallow optimization should give way to better judgment.

## Layered Comprehension Model
The same system should read differently as understanding grows. Age and comprehension are related but not identical: a very young player may stay at Wonder in one system and reach Strategy in another, while an older player may still prefer simpler readings.

- **Wonder**: The player sees immediate cause and effect. The tree gives fruit, the pie sells, the bridge changes shape, the village reacts.
- **Planning**: The player understands short sequences and delayed reward. If I save now, I can get something better later.
- **Strategy**: The player compares options and opportunity costs. Which use of time, goods, or money helps most right now and next?
- **Systems**: The player understands how production, ownership, projects, places, and people interact. Choices are no longer isolated; they alter a network.
- **Legacy**: The player thinks in terms of enduring patterns. What kind of life, household, and town am I building over time, and what keeps mattering after the moment passes?

## Ownership Model
- **Personal**: carried goods, personal tools, personal earnings, and personal ambitions belong here. This layer teaches agency, accountability, and the reality that individual choices matter.
- **Shared Household**: home storage, family-use tools, shared assets, and household improvements belong here. This layer teaches cooperation, negotiation, and the idea that not all value is purely individual.
- **Town / Civic**: bridges, commons, public workshops, district improvements, and other shared infrastructure belong here. This layer teaches that good life depends partly on goods no single person should own outright.

These layers matter because the game is not only about having things. It is about learning what should be kept, shared, invested, or given for the sake of a better future.

## Economy Constitution
The economy exists to convert work and judgment into better life. It must consistently answer the question: what can this money now make possible that was previously out of reach?

What must stay legible:
- where value comes from
- what something costs
- what is being traded away
- what an asset changes over time
- what a public contribution changes in the world

What should remain tangible early:
- physical goods
- visible coins
- simple prices
- productive assets
- concrete places of trade and craft
- public projects with visible progress

What should be avoided for a long time:
- abstract finance layers disconnected from place
- passive number-go-up economies
- opaque market simulation
- debt-heavy systems
- tax systems
- complex speculation mechanics
- anything that makes money itself feel like the final prize

Money must stay a means. If a feature makes currency feel more important than capability, beauty, freedom, or shared life, it violates the constitution.

## Society Constitution
Society in this game must be learned by living inside interdependence. Public goods should require real contribution, create visible-world consequence, and improve a place that players can return to and feel.

Visible consequence is mandatory. A funded bridge, repaired market, expanded commons, or improved district must change access, workflow, atmosphere, or opportunity in a way players can notice without explanation.

Society should be felt through places, projects, and mutual dependence rather than through lectures. The player should learn what a society is by helping build one.

## NPC Constitution
NPCs exist to make judgment social. They should ask for things, offer perspectives, react to player choices, surface trade-offs, and give the town human texture.

NPCs are not there to dump lore, explain the theme at length, flatter the player constantly, or simulate advanced artificial minds for their own sake. They should be memorable because they embody needs, values, and consequences, not because they monopolize attention.

NPCs should reflect the world back to the player. They should help players notice what changed, what was sacrificed, what mattered, and what kind of person or town is emerging.

## Expansion Rules
1. Expand by adding new content inside the permanent verbs, entities, and loop families rather than by replacing them.
2. Add new districts as new local expressions of the same game, each with its own places, villagers, assets, projects, commissions, and dilemmas.
3. Add professions as different ways to gather, make, trade, save, own, contribute, and judge, not as isolated minigames with separate values.
4. Add projects that produce visible shared consequences in real places, not abstract town-score increases.
5. Add information layers and dilemmas that deepen judgment without breaking baseline readability for a child.
6. Prefer sideways growth over vertical invalidation. New content should open different choices, not simply render earlier content trivial.
7. Early districts, goods, and assets must remain meaningful. They may become humble, but they must not become fake, useless, or spiritually obsolete.

## Breaking Change Rules
A conceptual breaking change is any change that forces the game to stop being about visible value creation, delayed gratification, productive ownership, judgment, and shared life.

Examples of breaking changes:
- making money accumulation the main end state instead of a means to better living
- replacing visible civic projects with abstract progress meters detached from place
- turning productive assets into passive rewards with no stewardship or consequence
- making later content strictly obsolete earlier content instead of recontextualizing it
- shifting the game toward public-online-first competition, global markets, or MMO logic
- turning NPCs into exposition machines or entertainment companions with no real social function
- introducing complexity that hides cause and effect before the player has mastered the basics

If a change requires the old game to be mentally discarded instead of extended, it is a breaking change.

## Feature Acceptance Test
Every future feature must answer these questions clearly:

- Which permanent verb does it strengthen?
- Which core loop family does it belong to?
- Which comprehension tier or tiers does it serve?
- What real trade-off does it create?
- What visible consequence does it produce in life, place, relationship, or future possibility?
- Is it additive, or does it disrupt and devalue earlier content?

If these answers are weak, vague, or purely abstract, the feature is not ready.

## Explicit Anti-Goals
- The game should not become shallow edutainment that lectures instead of simulating.
- The game should not become a manipulative retention machine built on compulsion loops.
- The game should not become public-online-first, competitive-first, or stranger-social-first.
- The game should not become an abstract finance simulator before place, craft, and society are deeply felt.
- The game should not become a pile of disconnected minigames that share assets but not meaning.
- The game should not become a lore-delivery vehicle where systems are secondary.
- The game should not become an advanced-AI character sandbox that distracts from the village itself.

## Open Tensions to Keep Watching
- magical wonder vs grounded realism
- simplicity vs depth
- child readability vs adult meaning
- cozy tone vs meaningful scarcity
- personal ownership vs shared obligation
- stable rules vs surprising dilemmas

## Final Rule
When uncertain, choose the option that makes better judgment visibly improve lived life.
