# Core Ontology

## Purpose
This document defines the stable world grammar of MargotGameOfLife. The design team should use it to classify new content, resolve ambiguity, and expand the game by adding new instances and combinations of existing categories instead of inventing new world classes casually.

## Ontology Design Rule
Future content should mostly be new instances and combinations of stable categories, not new categories. A new category needs strong reason: it must solve repeated classification problems across many districts, professions, and years of expansion.

## Category Overview
- Actors
- Objects
- Structured Activities
- Places and World Structures
- Time-Based Structures
- Information Structures
- Ownership and Scope Layers

## Actors
### Player
What it is: a primary human-controlled actor in the world.  
Systemic role: makes choices, owns things, contributes to projects, and carries consequence.  
What it is not: a detached camera, a god-system, or a purely narrative protagonist.

### Co-op Partner
What it is: a second primary human-controlled actor sharing the world.  
Systemic role: turns planning, ownership, work, and trade-offs into shared decision-making.  
What it is not: an NPC helper, background account, or abstract household bonus.

### Villager
What it is: a persistent local social actor with needs, opinions, routines, and perspective.  
Systemic role: gives the town its human texture through requests, trade, feedback, and consequence.  
What it is not: a lore terminal, quest dispenser by default, or advanced-AI spectacle.

### Specialist
What it is: a villager whose identity is anchored in a domain such as baking, building, milling, or carpentry.  
Systemic role: opens deeper craft, trade, commissions, upgrades, and judgments within that domain.  
What it is not: a skill tree menu, detached service kiosk, or separate species of actor.

### Companion
What it is: a persistent low-authority social presence attached to a player, household, or place.  
Systemic role: supports routine, observation, light help, and emotional continuity without replacing judgment.  
What it is not: the main social backbone of the village, a full co-op partner, or a high-complexity AI system.

### Visitor / Trader / Guest
What it is: a temporary or semi-temporary outside actor entering the local world.  
Systemic role: brings new offers, demands, information, pressure, and contrast without redefining the village.  
What it is not: the persistent center of town life or a stand-in for public-online multiplayer.

## Objects
Object categories are functional. One object may carry more than one classification when that is the clearest model.

### Resource
What it is: a raw or lightly processed input gathered from the world or produced by an asset.  
What function it serves: feeds recipes, trade, contributions, and planning.  
What differentiates it: it is primarily an input, not a finished solution.

### Crafted Good
What it is: an object produced by transforming inputs through a recipe or other making process.  
What function it serves: creates higher value, utility, beauty, or specificity than its inputs.  
What differentiates it: it is defined by transformation, not by whether it is consumed.

### Consumable
What it is: an object used up in order to satisfy a need, restore a condition, or trigger a short-lived effect.  
What function it serves: turns stored value into immediate benefit.  
What differentiates it: it disappears or is materially spent through use.

### Tool
What it is: an object used to improve, enable, or alter an action.  
What function it serves: changes efficiency, quality, access, safety, or possibility when gathering, making, maintaining, or moving.  
What differentiates it: it modifies action rather than being the main output of action.

### Decoration
What it is: an object whose primary role is aesthetic, symbolic, or status-bearing.  
What function it serves: changes beauty, identity, atmosphere, and social meaning.  
What differentiates it: its main value is expressive rather than productive.

### Productive Asset
What it is: an owned object or world thing that generates recurring value, capacity, or access over time.  
What function it serves: changes tomorrow by improving future output, convenience, freedom, or resilience.  
What differentiates it: it persists and keeps mattering beyond one immediate use.

### Civic Asset
What it is: a shared persistent structure or good that benefits a household cluster, district, or town.  
What function it serves: improves common life through access, safety, beauty, work capacity, or public usefulness.  
What differentiates it: its primary beneficiary is the shared world, not a single owner.

## Structured Activities
### Recipe
A recipe is a repeatable transformation of inputs, place, time, and effort into outputs. It is about making, not about why the making is requested.

### Trade
Trade is a structured exchange between actors or institutions where value moves in known terms. It expects reciprocal return rather than shared sacrifice.

### Commission
A commission is a request that connects an actor's need to production, delivery, judgment, or timing. It is more specific and relational than generic trade, and it is not a separate quest ontology.

### Upgrade
An upgrade is a structured improvement to an object, place, asset, or civic structure that leaves it durably better than before. It is about advancement, not preservation.

### Contribution
A contribution is the transfer of goods, labor, or priority into a shared household or civic outcome. It is not purchased reciprocity; it advances a common good.

### Maintenance
Maintenance is repeated work that preserves function, yield, safety, or appearance over time. It prevents decay or loss rather than creating a new category of thing.

### Experiment / Test
An experiment or test is a bounded activity whose primary output is information. It helps players learn uncertain rules, quality differences, timing, or trustworthiness without needing a separate science game.

## Places and World Structures
### Home
Home is the persistent domestic anchor of personal and shared household life. It holds daily routines, storage, identity, and household decisions.

### Workshop
A workshop is a place organized around making, repair, or transformation. Its identity comes from craft capacity, not from being merely indoors.

### Market
A market is a place where trade, price comparison, requests, and social-economic contact concentrate. It is a trade environment, not just a decorative plaza.

### District
A district is a cluster of places, actors, assets, opportunities, and civic conditions that reads as one local area. It is the main unit of additive world growth.

### Project Site
A project site is a place currently under shared work. It exists while a project is active and makes progress legible in the world.

### Civic Structure
A civic structure is a completed shared-world place or structure that serves common life. It is usually the durable result of a project.

### Access Structure
An access structure is a structure whose primary meaning is movement, connection, or route change, such as a bridge, road, dock, gate, or path improvement. Many access structures are also civic structures.

Places and world structures persist as spatial anchors. Activities happen at them, assets live in them, actors gather around them, and visible consequence should change them.

## Time-Based Structures
### Event
An event is a temporary world condition, gathering, pressure, or occasion that changes priorities, mood, or opportunity for a limited time. It overlays the world; it does not replace the underlying ontology.

### Yield Cycle
A yield cycle is the recurring timing by which a resource source or productive asset becomes ready again. It makes time materially meaningful without turning the game into abstract scheduling.

### Project Stage
A project stage is a discrete step in shared work that marks visible progress toward a civic result. It is the lifecycle of a project, not a separate class of project.

### Availability Window
An availability window is a bounded period when an activity, actor, place, or object can be meaningfully accessed. It is about access timing, not world identity.

### Seasonal Pattern
A seasonal pattern is a broad recurring rhythm that shapes yields, events, appearance, and opportunity across places. It should deepen planning without making the world unreadable.

### Reputation Shift
A reputation shift is a durable change in how one actor, household, or local public reads another. Trust belongs here as a relationship state, not as a currency or free-floating resource.

Time should change pacing, opportunity, and meaning while keeping world categories stable. Time alters the state of things; it should rarely create entirely new kinds of things.

## Information Structures
### Observation
An observation is directly perceived information from the world. It is the cleanest basis for judgment because the player can see it happen.

### Hint
A hint is partial guidance that points toward a possibility without proving it. It helps learning without replacing discovery.

### Rumor
A rumor is socially transmitted, uncertain information. It may be true, false, biased, or incomplete, and it exists to support judgment under uncertainty.

### Feedback
Feedback is a response to player action from actors, places, systems, or outcomes. It makes consequence legible but is not the same as proof.

### Record / Ledger
A record or ledger is durable stored information about transactions, ownership, contributions, deliveries, or prior outcomes. It stabilizes memory and supports longer-term planning.

### Visible Consequence
A visible consequence is a world change that shows what prior action accomplished, damaged, or revealed. It is the strongest form of truth in the game because it can be revisited.

Information structures should help players think. They should not replace thinking with omniscient explanation.

## Ownership and Scope Layers
- **Personal scope**: owned or controlled by one player. Typical examples are carried goods, personal tools, personal savings, and a personally owned tree.
- **Shared Household scope**: jointly used or jointly governed by the household. Typical examples are home storage, shared tools, family assets, and household upgrades.
- **Town / Civic scope**: held for common use by a district or town. Typical examples are bridges, commons, public workshops, roads, and squares.

The same category may appear in different scopes. A tree may be personal or shared household. An oven may be personal, shared, or civic depending on who controls it. A bridge is civic by default because its meaning is shared access.

Scope is not flavor. It determines who benefits, who decides, who maintains, and what kind of consequence the thing should produce.

## Relationship Rules
- Resources are transformed by recipes into crafted goods, consumables, upgrades, or contributions.
- Tools change how gathering, making, maintenance, and movement work; they rarely replace those activities.
- Productive assets generate recurring value through yield cycles, access improvement, capacity improvement, or convenience over time.
- Civic assets are usually the completed outcomes of contributions and project stages.
- Commissions connect actor needs to production, delivery, timing, and judgment.
- Trade converts goods, labor, or access into new options; it should not erase where value came from.
- Maintenance preserves the usefulness of assets, places, and civic structures so ownership and stewardship stay meaningful.
- Districts group places, actors, projects, events, information, and opportunities into a stable local context.
- Events and seasonal patterns change timing, pressure, and opportunity without redefining the underlying world categories.
- Observations, rumors, hints, feedback, records, and visible consequences shape judgment under uncertainty.
- Reputation shifts change how actors trade, request, warn, trust, or cooperate with one another.
- Objects, places, and activities may combine categories when that makes the world clearer; combinations are preferred over inventing special one-off types.

## Canonical Classification Examples
- **Apple**: `Resource`; also `Consumable` when eaten. It is a gathered input first and an edible thing second.
- **Pie**: `Crafted Good` and `Consumable`. It is made through transformation and can be eaten or sold.
- **Oven**: `Tool`; sometimes also `Productive Asset` when owned as a persistent capacity-expanding fixture. Its main meaning is enabling better making.
- **Apple tree**: `Productive Asset`. It creates recurring value through a `Yield Cycle` by producing apples over time.
- **Orchard**: primarily a `Place`; secondarily a concentration of `Productive Assets`. Default to place first unless ownership and yield are tracked at orchard level.
- **Bridge foundation**: `Project Site` plus current `Project Stage`. It is not yet a civic asset because it does not yet provide shared access.
- **Completed bridge**: `Civic Asset` and `Access Structure`. It is the durable shared result of a project and changes movement for everyone.
- **Market stall**: `Place`; when owned, also a `Productive Asset`. It is not a separate ontology type called "business" by default.
- **Village square**: `Place`; often also a `Civic Structure`. Its meaning comes from being a shared social center.
- **Baker request**: `Commission` issued by a `Specialist`. It may lead to `Trade`, but its identity is a need-directed request.
- **Harvest festival**: `Event`; it may express a `Seasonal Pattern` or celebrate a project outcome, but event is its primary class.
- **Trust with the builder**: `Reputation Shift` between actors. It is a relationship state that influences cooperation, information quality, and access, not a spendable resource.

## Ontology Boundaries
Do not create new ontology categories for every feature idea. Many future features should be combinations of existing categories.

Things that should usually not become standalone ontology categories:
- "business" when `Place` plus `Productive Asset` plus `Trade` already explains it
- "quest" when `Commission`, `Contribution`, or `Upgrade` already explains it
- "relationship currency" when `Reputation Shift` already explains it
- "festival system" when `Event` plus `District` plus `Visible Consequence` already explains it
- emotional themes such as trust, envy, pride, or gratitude as object types
- one-off mechanics that only exist for a single feature or district

If a concept can be described cleanly as a thing, a place, an activity, a time structure, an information structure, or a scope layer already in the ontology, do not mint a new class for it.

## Expansion Rules
- Add new professions by combining existing actor roles, tools, recipes, commissions, assets, workshops, and information structures.
- Add new districts by introducing new places, specialists, civic structures, project sites, events, and local reputation pressures inside the same ontology.
- Add new projects by reusing contribution, project stage, visible consequence, and civic asset logic rather than inventing a new project ontology each time.
- Add new dilemmas by changing information quality, timing, scarcity, trade-offs, and relationship consequences, not by adding abstract morality systems.
- Add new content at the level players can see and name in-world. Child-legible world things should stay primary.
- Prefer category combinations over category proliferation. If something only matters because of who owns it, when it appears, or what state it is in, model that through scope and time before inventing a new type.

## Smell Tests
- A new category is proposed because a feature "feels special."
- A category has only one likely example in the whole game.
- The category is really a mood, theme, or UI label rather than a world thing.
- The proposal turns a one-off mechanic into a permanent world class.
- The feature becomes harder to explain to a bright child after the new category is introduced.
- The same result could be explained more cleanly by combining existing categories.
- The design starts talking in abstractions players will never see in-world.

## Final Rule
Prefer clearer combinations over new kinds of things.
