# Lore Constitution

## Purpose
This document exists to protect the long-term lore identity of MargotGameOfLife. It defines the permanent tonal rules, mystery boundaries, and world-facing lore logic so the setting can deepen for years without becoming childish dead-weight, decorative fantasy wallpaper, or an exposition burden.

## Core Lore Rule
Lore must deepen the same world over time rather than replacing it with different age-bracketed settings. The world a 4-year-old loves first should still be the true world later, only better understood.

## One-Sentence World Thesis
MargotGameOfLife takes place in a warm, living world where places, creatures, craft, and old works quietly remember care and visibly respond to how people live within them.

## Tone Pillars
- **Cozy wonder**: the world should feel inviting, beautiful, and easy to love before it feels deep.
- **Ancient depth**: the world should feel older than the player's understanding, with traces of prior care and prior making.
- **Emotional safety**: mystery and consequence should feel real without making the world threatening, cruel, or horror-forward.
- **Living places**: places should feel alive with memory, rhythm, and response rather than like static scenery.
- **Meaningful care**: tending, repairing, feeding, growing, and contributing should feel naturally important in the world.
- **Gentle mystery**: the world should always suggest one layer more than it fully explains.

## What the World Must Feel Like
First, a young child should feel: this place is beautiful, creatures are interesting, people need each other, and good care makes things better. The world should feel safe to explore, satisfying to help, and rewarding to notice.

Later, deeper layers should suggest: old places carry memory, craft traditions matter, some wonders are sleeping rather than gone, and wise judgment means learning how care, neglect, beauty, scarcity, and shared life shape the future.

## What the World Is Made Of
- **Creatures**: living presences that make places feel inhabited, surprising, and relational.
- **Villages and households**: the human center of daily life, work, custom, and interdependence.
- **Cultivated places**: orchards, gardens, fields, ponds, groves, and other cared-for natural spaces where life and labor meet.
- **Craft traditions**: ovens, mills, workshops, tools, recipes, and the habits of making useful and beautiful things well.
- **Routes and crossings**: bridges, paths, roads, gates, docks, and other connectors that make shared life physically possible.
- **Old works and traces**: worn structures, overgrown foundations, faded markers, old murals, forgotten platforms, and half-remembered built things that invite restoration.
- **Sleeping or restorable wonders**: places or structures that feel dormant, hidden, quiet, or incomplete rather than destroyed beyond meaning.

These motifs must keep serving play. They are not decorative categories; they are the recurring world forms through which gathering, making, saving, restoring, contributing, and judging become meaningful.

## What Magic Means in This World
Magic in this world is not arbitrary chaos and not primarily a spell-combat system. It is the subtle aliveness of places, creatures, old works, materials, and traditions when they are approached with attention, right use, patience, and care.

Magic should feel:
- place-bound more often than free-floating
- relational more often than dominating
- discovered more often than announced
- awakened more often than invented from nothing

Magic may gather around certain creatures, routes, ruins, customs, gardens, tools, or acts of restoration. It should make the world feel deeper and more responsive, not less legible.

## Creature Constitution
Creatures exist to make the world feel alive, lovable, and deeper than utility alone. They are sources of wonder, companionship, ecological meaning, and place-based mystery.

They are not primarily for battle-collection gameplay, roster completion, stat obsession, or franchise-style power escalation. A creature should matter because of where it belongs, how it behaves, what it responds to, and how it changes the feeling of a place.

Creature principles:
- creatures should feel attached to habitats, routes, seasons, customs, or specific kinds of care
- rarity should come from relationship, place, timing, or wonder, not only from scarcity tables
- companionship should feel affectionate and meaningful without becoming the whole game
- rare luminous, graceful, or unicorn-like beings may exist, but they should feel dignified, surprising, and tied to the world rather than sugary or ornamental
- creatures should deepen stewardship and observation, not pull the game away from craft, society, and restoration

## Ruins, Old Works, and Ancient Traces
This world contains old bridges, paths, workshops, gardens, towers, platforms, mills, shrines, and other traces because people cared for this world before the player arrived. The world has layers of prior making, prior routes, and prior attention.

These old works should usually feel worn, sleeping, overgrown, incomplete, or half-understood rather than ruined by endless war or grim catastrophe. Their main purpose is not backstory spectacle. Their purpose is to make restoration, rediscovery, and renewed use feel natural.

The mystery of old works should stay open-ended. The player should often feel that something mattered here before, that it may matter again, and that care can reconnect the present to what was left behind.

## Care, Craft, and Restoration
Care is world-authentic in MargotGameOfLife because this world is built to answer attention. Feeding, repairing, planting, cleaning, maintaining, baking, mending, rebuilding, and contributing should feel like real ways of participating in how the world stays alive.

Craft is not just manufacturing. It is a respectful relationship between materials, traditions, place, and need. Restoration is not just fixing damage. It is the recovery of usefulness, beauty, access, and shared meaning.

The world should visibly answer care and visibly reveal neglect, but this should not be framed as a scolding moral meter. The point is that the world is responsive, not that it is constantly judging the player.

## Society and Shared Life
This is a world of villages, households, trades, gatherings, local sayings, seasonal routines, shared projects, and practical customs. Society should feel small enough to know, rich enough to matter, and concrete enough to see.

At the lore level, shared life should feel rooted in:
- people relying on each other's work
- places shaped by repeated use
- customs that help people remember how to live well together
- gatherings that renew belonging
- public works that make common life possible

Avoid abstract political exposition. The player should feel society through who lives here, what they tend, what they repair, what they celebrate, and what they build together.

## Mystery Rules
The right kinds of mystery for this world are:
- old purposes that are only partly understood
- creatures whose behavior hints at deeper patterns
- local customs whose meaning unfolds over time
- murals, sayings, or objects that suggest prior care
- places that seem asleep, hidden, misused, or waiting
- routes, gardens, or structures that connect present life to older layers

The wrong kinds of mystery for this world are:
- horror-forward dread
- gruesome hidden history
- giant war chronologies
- plot-twist conspiracies as the main attraction
- lore that only works after reading large text dumps
- mystery built from withholding basic legibility

Mystery should invite return, not fear. It should make the world feel older and deeper than the player currently understands without making the player feel locked out of belonging.

## Layered Comprehension of Lore
- **Wonder**: the player sees a lovely creature, a strange old bridge, a glowing garden corner, or a quiet mural and feels curiosity and affection first.
- **Planning**: the player starts to understand that places respond to care, creatures respond to conditions, and old works may matter again if tended properly.
- **Strategy**: the player sees that lore matters for decisions because routes, customs, requests, creature habits, and restorable places affect better choices.
- **Systems**: the player understands that creatures, craft, projects, memory, place, and social trust form one living world rather than separate content buckets.
- **Legacy**: the player feels that repeated care reconnects present life to older layers and helps decide what kind of village, household, and world will continue.

## Lore Delivery Rules
Lore should be delivered mainly through:
- places
- repairs
- objects
- murals
- sayings
- creature behavior
- festivals
- village customs
- visible changes in the world

Lore should also be delivered through what becomes possible after care, restoration, or contribution. The strongest lore delivery is a changed place the player can revisit.

Discourage:
- giant exposition dumps
- codex overload
- backstory-first design
- dialogue that explains everything
- naming many grand eras, factions, or myth systems before the player can feel the world itself

## Expansion Rules
New districts, creatures, professions, projects, customs, and mysteries should deepen this same world by adding new places, new traces, new patterns of care, and new layers of meaning.

Expansion should mostly work by:
- showing different local expressions of living places
- revealing new kinds of creature-place relationship
- adding old works that can be restored or better understood
- expanding craft traditions and village customs
- introducing new mysteries that remain emotionally safe and place-based

Early lore must not become childish dead-weight later. The orchard, bridge, baker, builder, and first creature wonders should still feel true in a later deeper world, not like disposable beginner fluff.

## Explicit Anti-Goals
- generic fantasy kingdom lore
- grimdark myth history
- collectible creature combat franchise imitation
- overcomplicated cosmology
- babyish magical toyland
- text-heavy worldbuilding disconnected from play
- giant named backstory burdens
- combat-first fantasy as the main identity of the setting

## Open Tensions to Keep Watching
- softness vs depth
- mystery vs clarity
- child safety vs real consequence
- magic vs system legibility
- creature charm vs economy and craft focus
- ancient depth vs overexplained history

## Final Rule
Let the world grow deeper by becoming more cared for, not more explained.
